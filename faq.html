Straight answers about what this is, what it isn't, and how it works — without spin.

◆ The short version
This is a non-partisan, costed, sourced policy framework, open for any candidate to adopt in whole or in part. It is not a party, not a slate, and not for sale. Everything below elaborates on that. If your question isn't here, write to info@acitythatworks.ca and we'll answer it — and probably add it.

◆ The Basics
What is A City That Works, in one sentence?

It is an independent, non-partisan policy framework for the City of Victoria — roughly 101 costed, sourced, measurable measures across five pillars plus a foundation — published openly for any candidate or resident to use, adapt, or challenge ahead of the October 17, 2026 municipal election.

Is this a political party?

No. There is no party, no membership, no whip, no caucus, and no leader. It is a public document. Anyone can read it, adopt parts of it, argue with it, or ignore it. A party disciplines its members to vote a certain way; this framework has no members and disciplines no one.

Is this a candidate slate?

No. A slate is a group of candidates who run together, coordinate a campaign, and share infrastructure. This framework does none of that. Multiple candidates may independently endorse the same measures — that is the point — but they are not running together, do not coordinate through this document, and owe it nothing beyond whatever they choose to adopt publicly.

Who is behind it?

The framework was drafted by residents working from publicly available research, municipal data, council records, and peer-city evidence. It is intended to stand on the quality of its measures and its sources, not on the identity of its authors. Corrections and contributions come from anyone with relevant expertise — planners, police, builders, social workers, fiscal analysts, engineers.

What does "0% ideological, 100% pragmatic" actually mean?

It means the test for every measure is "does this work elsewhere and can it work here?" — not "does this fit a left or right label?" The framework deliberately absorbs strong ideas from across the spectrum: a progressive councillor's supportive-housing throughput program sits beside a fiscal-restraint councillor's enforcement-of-existing-rules approach, because both are evidence-backed and within municipal power. No measure is included or excluded on ideological grounds; it is included if it is costed, sourced, and deliverable, and excluded if it isn't.

What are the five pillars?
  • 🏠 A Liveable City — housing, families, childcare, cleanliness, mobility
  • 🛡️ A Safe City — public safety, order, accountability, modern infrastructure
  • 🌳 A Beautiful City — heritage, parks, urban aesthetics, climate resilience
  • 💰 A Well-Managed City — taxes, permits, performance, fiscal discipline
  • 🗳️ A Democratic City — referendums, transparency, participation, public-visualization tools

Plus a Foundation, not a pillar — honouring the host Nations (Songhees and Esquimalt), built into how the City operates rather than how it speaks.

◆ Is It Legitimate? The Skeptic's Section
This sounds like a stealth campaign. Isn't it really just one candidate's platform?

No — and the framework is structured specifically to prevent that. It carries no candidate's name, endorses no candidate, and is explicitly open to every candidate in the race, including incumbents from across the current council. If it were one person's platform, it would not invite rivals to use it freely. The goal is to raise the standard of debate for everyone, which only works if it belongs to no one.

Who paid for this? Is there money behind it?

The framework is a document, not a campaign. It does not raise or spend funds to support or oppose candidates, and it does not run election advertising. There is no ad budget, no donor pool attached to it, and nothing for sale. If that changes in any way that would engage BC's election-finance rules, it will be disclosed.

Does this trigger Elections BC third-party advertiser rules?

The framework itself does not engage in election advertising — it does not spend money to promote or oppose candidates. It publishes policy research and a passive endorsement registry. That said, any candidate who uses framework language in their own paid campaign materials is responsible for their own compliance with the Local Elections Campaign Financing Act, and should confirm with their campaign's compliance lead. The framework cannot and does not provide legal advice.

Why should I trust the numbers?

Don't trust them — check them. Every measure carries a budget-impact estimate, and the Savings & Revenue Analysis shows the math with sources and explicit caveats, including where estimates are uncertain and where year-one delivery differs from steady-state. Where a peer-city claim is cited (Phoenix managed competition, Calgary's SAVE program, Pittsburgh's adaptive signals, Hoboken's Vision Zero record), the source is named so you can verify it. The framework has corrected its own numbers publicly when they were wrong — that track record is the point.

Has the framework ever admitted it got something wrong?

Yes, in writing and on the record. The budget analysis was revised downward when an earlier version under-counted the cost of its own additions. A flagship technology measure — a full city-scale "digital twin" — was demoted from a near-term commitment to a contingent, long-term aspiration after honest review concluded that no Canadian city of Victoria's size has successfully deployed one, and that cheaper tools capture most of the value. A framework that won't correct itself isn't pragmatic; it's just another platform.

Isn't "non-partisan" just cover for a particular political agenda?

A fair challenge, and the answer is in the measures themselves. The framework simultaneously calls for competitive bidding on some services (often coded "right") and a 50% cut in unsheltered homelessness through Housing-First with wraparound services (often coded "left"); for property-tax restraint and for protecting tenants from displacement; for enforcing existing bylaws and for capacity-first sequencing so enforcement isn't cruelty. If it had a hidden partisan agenda, it would be far more internally consistent in one direction. Its willingness to hold both is the evidence.

◆ What the City Can Actually Do
Half of these problems aren't even the City's job. Why promise on them?

The framework is unusually disciplined about this — it is built around what a municipality can actually deliver versus what it can only advocate for. Healthcare, drug policy, sentencing, major transit, and the building code are provincial or federal. Where a measure touches those, it is framed as advocate, partner, or co-fund, never deliver. There is a companion guide, How City Hall Actually Works, written specifically to help residents spot the difference and hold every candidate — including any who endorse this framework — to it.

Can the Mayor actually do most of this alone?

No, and the framework says so plainly. BC does not have a "strong mayor" system — the Mayor of Victoria has one vote of nine and chairs meetings, but cannot unilaterally hire, fire, sign contracts, or pass bylaws. Most measures require a council majority. This is why the framework is addressed to all candidates, not a single would-be mayor: who else sits on council matters as much as who holds the chair.

Victoria already does some of these things. Is the framework pretending otherwise?

No. Where the City already does something well — the AAA cycling network, the DVBA Clean Team, BC Housing partnerships, the Crystal Pool referendum, the Climate Leadership Plan — the framework explicitly says it will continue that work, not claim to invent it. The differentiation is in the measures the current council has not deployed: published permit benchmarks, competitive service benchmarking, a quarterly performance dashboard, rotating zero-based budget reviews, and a glide path back toward inflation-level tax increases.

◆ The Money Questions
How can you promise no service cuts AND lower tax increases?

It isn't free, and the framework doesn't pretend it is. The claim is narrower and honest: a glide path — Year 1 capped at ≤6.5% (versus the council's 10.44% draft), Year 2 ≤5%, Year 3 and beyond around inflation plus population growth (~3.5%). That is funded by operational savings (managed competition, zero-based reviews, administrative attrition through retirement not layoffs) plus new property-tax revenue from housing growth — not by cutting frontline services. Year-one savings are a fraction of steady-state, and the analysis states that openly. Anyone promising full savings on day one is misleading you.

"Find efficiencies" is what every politician says. How is this different?

Because the framework names the mechanism, not just the goal. "Managed competition for waste collection and street cleaning, targeting ~20% savings on ~$12M, with mandatory re-employment clauses protecting at least 80% of existing workers" is a mechanism. "Rotating zero-based reviews of 3–4 service areas per budget cycle" is a mechanism. "Reduce administrative headcount 10% over the term through attrition only" is a mechanism. Each has a peer-city precedent and a number attached. "Find efficiencies" with no method behind it is exactly what the framework rejects.

Isn't "managed competition" just privatization with a friendlier name?

No — and the distinction is built into the measure. Managed competition (on the Phoenix model) lets City workers bid against private firms for the work; when public delivery is competitive, it stays public. Winning private bidders must offer equivalent positions to at least 80% of affected City workers at equal or higher pay. The City keeps oversight and audits performance quarterly, and can re-tender if a contractor underperforms. The CRD's blue-box recycling is already contracted out — the principle is proven regionally. It is competition for the work, with worker protection, not a fire-sale of public assets.

Where can I see the full budget math?

In the Savings & Revenue Analysis. It separates operating savings from new revenue from capital debt service, distinguishes year-one from steady-state, chooses the conservative end of every range, and lists what it deliberately did not count as savings (behavioural-change guesses, parking-data licensing, unguaranteed grants). It also includes a section on how to stress-test the framework's own claims if you're a skeptic, journalist, or rival.

◆ For Candidates
I'm running. Can I use this?

Yes — that is the whole point. You can endorse the full framework, endorse specific pillars, or endorse individual measures, in your own words and with your own modifications. There is no exclusivity, no fee, and no permission required. The only ask is attribution where practical ("drawn from A City That Works") and, if you're elected, a commitment to publish quarterly variance reports against the measures you ran on. The Candidate Endorsement Pack has everything you need.

If I endorse, am I joining something? Do I owe anyone anything?

No. Endorsing creates no membership, no caucus obligation, and no coordination requirement. You do not owe the framework money, loyalty, or votes. You can endorse some measures and publicly oppose others. You can withdraw an endorsement at any time (it's logged, by date, like everything else). The relationship is one-way: candidates adopt the framework; the framework endorses no candidate back.

What if I endorse a measure and it turns out to be unworkable in office?

The framework expects this and gives you three honest options: refine it (keep the goal, change the method, publish what changed), substitute it (replace it with something that achieves the same outcome, and say so), or withdraw it (publicly, with an explanation). All three protect your credibility better than the usual option — quietly failing and hoping nobody notices.

Won't opponents attack me for "running on someone else's platform"?

The answer is simple and disarming: the framework is open to every candidate in the race, including your opponents. It's a public menu of evidence-based, costed proposals — your opponent could endorse the same measures tomorrow. Using rigorous, sourced research is not a weakness to apologize for; the weakness is running on aesthetics with no numbers behind them. The Candidate Endorsement Pack includes ready-to-use language for exactly this question.

I'm a sitting councillor. Is this for incumbents too?

Yes. Several measures are politically neutral and could be endorsed by sitting members from across the current council — quarterly dashboards, the Saanich Citizens' Assembly, the Indigenous working tables, the Integrity Commissioner. Measure-by-measure endorsement exists precisely so incumbents can adopt what fits their record without endorsing things they've voted against.

◆ For Residents
I'm not running for anything. What's in this for me?

Two things. First, a checklist for evaluating any candidate's promises — is it within municipal power, is it costed, is it sourced, is there a delivery date and an accountability mechanism (see How City Hall Actually Works). Second, a concrete list of measures you can ask your candidates about by name: "Do you support published permit-processing benchmarks? A quarterly performance dashboard? The tax glide path?" Specific questions get specific answers and expose vague ones.

How do I suggest a change or point out an error?

Write to info@acitythatworks.ca. Every measure is open to correction — a misread source, a math error, an assumption that doesn't hold, or a better idea. Revisions are dated and published. The framework treats being corrected as the system working, not as an attack.

What's the single best way to influence City Hall, framework or not?

Meeting your councillors individually about a specific issue, speaking at the statutorily required public hearing on land-use matters, and voting in every municipal election — where turnout is typically 35–45%, so each vote carries roughly three times the weight it does federally. None of it is glamorous; all of it works better than complaining online. The full list is in How City Hall Actually Works.

◆ Hot-Button Specifics
Are you putting surveillance in the streetlights?

No — the framework explicitly rules it out. The streetlight measure is LED conversion plus an IoT platform for outage detection and brightness control (the Kelowna/Halifax model), and it pre-emptively rejects audio surveillance, gunshot/scream detection, facial recognition, behavioural-pattern AI, and any "directed brightness at a suspect" function. The crime-reduction benefit of good lighting itself — about 21% at night per systematic-review evidence — is real and requires no surveillance. The framework is, as far as we know, the only platform in the race to take an explicit no-surveillance position.

Are you anti-environment or anti-business?

Neither, by design. The framework commits to a 50% community GHG reduction by 2030, 5,000 new trees, 60% non-auto mode share, and expanded home-retrofit support with published uptake numbers — that's results-oriented environmentalism, not symbolism. It also cuts red tape for small business, targets a 5-business-day licence turnaround, and protects the downtown that small operators depend on. Treating these as opposites is the old framing the whole document is built to reject.

Do you actually care about homelessness, or just about clearing it off the streets?

The framework's position is that compassion without accountability helps no one, and enforcement without capacity is cruelty. It commits to a 50% reduction in unsheltered homelessness through Housing-First, supportive housing, wraparound services, and a supportive-housing throughput program (so stabilized residents can move on and free up units), with published outcome metrics — not just dollars spent. Its sequencing is explicit: build capacity first, then enforce, and never "move people along" to nowhere. It also flags that Victoria carries a wildly disproportionate share of the region's shelter and supportive housing and calls for the other municipalities to take their fair share.

What happened to the "digital twin" idea I read about earlier?

It was honestly downgraded. Earlier versions proposed a full city-scale digital twin as a near-term project. On review, the framework concluded that no Canadian municipality of Victoria's size (~92,000 residents) has successfully deployed one, that the cost-benefit didn't hold at this scale, and that cheaper tools capture most of the value. So the full digital twin became a long-term, contingent aspiration, and was replaced with three smaller, peer-precedented measures: modernizing the City's underground-utility GIS data, AI-generated public-consultation imagery (continuing a sitting councillor's precedent), and a one-time LIDAR scan of the downtown core.

Why $25 million as the referendum threshold?

It's the line above which a new (non-lifecycle) capital project goes to a binding public vote, within the BC Local Government Act and Community Charter framework. The Crystal Pool replacement (approved February 2025, 58.7% Yes) is the proof of concept — the framework simply makes that the standard rule rather than a one-off. Routine lifecycle work — replacing aging pipes, resurfacing roads, renewing water and sewer — is explicitly excluded, because subjecting essential maintenance to a referendum would be absurd. It's a handful of big new-build decisions per term, not every park bench.
