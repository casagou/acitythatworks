A Citizens' Framework◆Victoria, BC · 2026◆v1.3 · May 27, 2026

A city that works.

Security. Cleanliness. Results.

What do you expect for Victoria?

A City That Works is an independent, non-partisan policy framework for the City of Victoria, drafted by residents who want practical results from their local government.

The framework is 0% ideological, 100% pragmatic. Its only loyalty is to what works elsewhere and can work here: housing that gets built, streets that feel safe, parks that are beautiful, budgets that are honest, and a city government that earns trust by measuring what it does and reporting back every quarter.

This is not a party. There are no whips, no candidate slate, no exclusivity — just a public menu of evidence-based municipal policies, costed and sourced, open for adoption by any candidate.

◆ How this framework was built

0% ideology. 100% pragmatic.

We started where the truth is most visible: the budget. Politicians make promises; numbers don't lie. Victoria's operating budget grew 29% in three years, from $297.9M to $384.1M proposed — with no competitive benchmarking, no AI, no smart infrastructure deployed. For every problem a Victorian faces daily, we applied the same six-step method.

01
Identify the root cause
Not the symptom — the structural reason the problem exists.
02
Assess what the City can do
Municipalities have specific powers. Don't promise what requires provincial or federal action.
03
Study what works elsewhere
Pittsburgh's Surtrac: −25% travel time. Phoenix managed competition: $25M+ saved 1978–88. Calgary SAVE: $74M targeted, $60M found.
04
Measure with technology
Use data, sensors, and digital tools to understand before deciding.
05
Build solutions with practitioners
Engineers, police, builders, social workers, business owners, residents.
06
Cost every measure
Direct and indirect impacts. Every proposal in this framework has a budget impact estimate.
◆ The 5-Step Process

No major new public project without these five steps.

The Dowler Place file proved why this matters. Under this framework, no project skips any step.

STEP 01
Local consultation
Problems, pain points, what needs to improve.
STEP 02
Expert design
Engineers, planners, affected residents.
STEP 03
Visualize & analyze
AI imagery (M53b) + GIS analysis (M53). See the future before deciding.
STEP 04
Referendum if > $25M
Binding public vote on new capital projects.
STEP 05
Quarterly reports
On time, on budget, or explain why not.
◆ Separation of Powers

What the City actually controls.

Before making promises, we must be honest about what the City of Victoria actually controls. We advocate aggressively to senior governments for action in their domains — but we never promise what isn't ours to deliver.

City of Victoria controls
Land use & zoning · building permits · property taxes · bylaws & enforcement · municipal policing (via VicPD Board) · parks & recreation · local roads & sidewalks · water & sewer · garbage collection · local planning · business licences · public art and heritage designation
Province of BC controls
Healthcare · K-12 education · social housing funding (BC Housing) · welfare & income assistance · courts & sentencing · major transit (BC Transit) · highways · building code · Residential Tenancy Act
Federal government controls
Immigration · criminal law · employment insurance · OAS · major infrastructure grants · Indigenous affairs · ports & harbours · motor vehicle safety standards

◆ Principles

What we are — and what we're not.

We are
  • →Govern like managers — measure, adjust, deliver
  • →Consult before building — and listen to the answer
  • →Visualize before constructing — AI imagery + GIS analysis
  • →Protect the environment with results, not slogans
  • →Enforce the rules that exist — before inventing new ones
  • →Respect taxpayers — every dollar audited, every quarter reported
  • →Cost every promise — no measure without a budget line
We aren't
  • →We don't do ideology — we do results
  • →We don't make promises we can't measure
  • →We don't pick fights — we fix problems
  • →We don't spend first and justify later
  • →We don't try things in real life when we can simulate first
  • →We don't deploy public surveillance — better lighting, not behavioural AI
