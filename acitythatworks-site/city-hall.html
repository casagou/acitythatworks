How City Hall Actually Works

A plain-language guide to municipal power in Victoria. Read it once, and you'll spot the impossible promises faster than most journalists.

← Back to the framework
◆ Why this document exists
Every election cycle, candidates make promises the City cannot keep. The result is the same: a council elected on commitments it cannot deliver, four years of frustration, and a city that gets harder to govern. This page exists so you can do better. It is reference material — true regardless of which candidates win in October 2026.

The 30-Second Version

Canada has three orders of government. Each does different things. Most political frustration in your daily life is the result of someone in the wrong order trying to fix — or being blamed for not fixing — something that belongs to a different order.

OrderDoesExamples relevant to you
FederalNational-level mattersCriminal Code, immigration, employment insurance, ports & harbours, Indigenous Crown-Nation relationships, federal infrastructure grants
Provincial (BC)Health, education, housing finance, courts, highways, most social servicesBC Housing, healthcare, K-12 schools, BC Transit, courts & sentencing, building code, Speculation & Vacancy Tax
Municipal (City of Victoria)Land use, local infrastructure, local policing, parks, garbage, local bylawsZoning, building permits, property tax, VicPD, parks, sidewalks, bylaws, local water & sewer

Your Mayor and Councillors operate at the third level only. They have no formal authority to change federal criminal law, set provincial healthcare policy, build provincial highways, or run the school system. They can advocate — sometimes effectively — but they cannot deliver those things unilaterally.

A candidate who promises to "end the opioid crisis" is either misinformed or counting on you to be. The same is true of "fix homelessness," "reform sentencing," or "force BC Transit to expand the Westshore line." The most effective Mayors are the ones who understand the actual lane and use every centimetre of it.

What the City Actually Controls

These are areas where Victoria City Council has direct authority. A measure within these categories is something a Mayor and Council can deliver through ordinary municipal process.

Land Use and Zoning

The single most powerful municipal tool. The Council decides what can be built where, how tall, with what setbacks, parking requirements, and design standards. Through the Official Community Plan (OCP) and zoning bylaws, the Council shapes the physical city for decades.

Within City authority: zoning bylaws, OCP, Heritage Conservation Areas, design guidelines, density bonuses, development cost charges, amenity cost charges, parking requirements. NOT within City authority: provincial Building Code, provincial Bill 44 (mandatory missing-middle minimums), Speculation & Vacancy Tax.

Property Tax

The Council sets the property tax rate annually through a Five-Year Financial Plan and Tax Rate Bylaw, both adopted by May 15. Property tax is the City's largest revenue source.

Within City authority: total tax levy, distribution between property classes, tax exemptions for non-profits and heritage properties, special assessment areas. NOT within City authority: BC Assessment property valuations (independent provincial agency), Speculation & Vacancy Tax rates, federal income tax.

Local Bylaws and Enforcement

Within City authority: noise bylaw, sign bylaw, business licence bylaw, streets and traffic bylaw, parks regulation bylaw, public nuisance bylaws. NOT within City authority: anything criminal (assault, theft, drug trafficking — federal Criminal Code), provincial offences, labour standards.

Local Infrastructure

The City owns and maintains local roads, sidewalks, water mains, sewer lines, stormwater pipes, parks, community centres, civic facilities, and the urban forest on public land. NOT within City authority: Trans-Canada Highway (provincial), the harbour (federal Crown), BC Ferries terminals (provincial Crown corporation), railways (federal), the airport (federal lease).

Local Policing (via VicPD Board)

Victoria has a municipal police force shared with Esquimalt, overseen by the Victoria and Esquimalt Police Board. The Mayor of Victoria chairs the Board. Within Police Board authority: hiring the Chief Constable, setting the police budget (subject to council approval), approving policies. NOT within authority: criminal sentencing, Crown prosecution decisions, parole, bail, conditions of remand custody — all federal or provincial.

Permits and Licensing

Within City authority: permit processing times, fees (within reasonable limits), application requirements, conditions of approval, inspection regimes. NOT within City authority: provincial Building Code, professional regulation of engineers and architects, BC Assessment classifications.

Parks, Recreation, and Culture

Within City authority: park operations, recreation programming, fees, library funding (with GVPL), public art, heritage designation of local buildings. NOT within City authority: Beacon Hill Park's specific trust restrictions, Royal BC Museum (provincial), provincial historic sites.

What the City Does NOT Control

These are areas where Council can advocate, lobby, partner, and sometimes co-fund — but cannot deliver unilaterally. Promises here should be treated with caution.

  • Healthcare & mental health — Island Health is provincial. The City cannot build a hospital, hire a doctor, or open a treatment bed.
  • Addiction services & drug policy — federal Criminal Code + provincial health authorities. The City's role is bylaw enforcement of public-space rules and outreach coordination.
  • Homelessness services — BC Housing funds and contracts most response. The City contributes land, fast-tracked permits, and partnership funding. Victoria provides 89% of the CRD's shelter beds and 83% of its supportive housing despite being one of 13 municipalities; the other 12 are under no legal obligation to take more.
  • Education (K-12) — School District 61 is independently elected; the Province sets curriculum and funds schools.
  • Major transit — BC Transit is a provincial Crown corporation. Westshore rapid transit requires provincial decision-making and funding.
  • Highways & major roads — the Trans-Canada, Pat Bay Highway, and Douglas north of city limits are provincial (MoTI).
  • Building Code — the BC Building Code (including the Zero Carbon Step Code) is provincial; the City enforces but cannot change it.
  • Vacancy & speculation taxes — provincial. A municipal version requires provincial enabling legislation that does not exist for commercial vacancy.
  • Federal criminal law & immigration — sentencing reform is federal; prosecution is the BC Prosecution Service; immigration is federal.
  • Indigenous Crown-Nation relationships — the formal relationship is federal. The City has operational working relationships but cannot resolve federal Crown obligations.

How City Hall Is Structured

The Council

  • 1 Mayor, elected at-large for a 4-year term
  • 8 Councillors, all elected at-large (no wards in Victoria), 4-year terms
  • Total of 9 votes on most matters; simple majority (5 of 9) carries
  • Some matters require a supermajority

The Mayor's Powers

BC does not have a "strong mayor" system. The Mayor of Victoria is first among equals, not a chief executive. The Mayor has one vote, chairs council and the Police Board, speaks for the City, and designates committee assignments — but cannot unilaterally hire, fire, sign contracts, pass bylaws, or override a Council majority. If you elect a Mayor on a platform the Council majority opposes, the Mayor will struggle to deliver it. Who else is on Council matters as much as who is Mayor.

Senior Staff

The Chief Administrative Officer (CAO) is the senior unelected official and the only direct hire of Council as a body. Department heads report to the CAO, not to individual councillors. Councillors cannot direct staff individually — they direct staff collectively through Council resolutions.

How Decisions Get Made

How a Bylaw Passes

Staff report → first reading → second reading → public hearing (required for zoning and OCP amendments) → third reading → adoption (a separate vote after at least one day's gap). The public hearing is the formal opportunity for residents to comment before Council votes on land use.

How the Budget Is Made

Draft Five-Year Financial Plan (fall) → provisional budget (Nov/Dec) → public engagement → deliberations (Jan–Apr) → Tax Rate Bylaw and Financial Plan Bylaw adopted by May 15 → tax notices issued, payable in July. The Province sets the May 15 deadline; Council cannot delay indefinitely.

Referendums

Two statutory pathways: Assent Voting (Local Government Act, Part 4) — a binding referendum, majority decides; and the Alternative Approval Process (AAP) (Community Charter) — the proposal proceeds unless at least 10% of eligible voters formally oppose it. The AAP is faster, used for routine borrowing. Assent Voting was used for the Crystal Pool referendum (February 2025, 58.7% Yes).

Special Governance Items

Victoria and Esquimalt Police Board

The Board oversees a police force serving two municipalities. Victoria pays 86.33% of the joint VicPD budget; Esquimalt pays the remainder. Disputes over the split have escalated to provincial appeals. The Police Board, not Council, has direct authority over the Chief Constable and policing operations.

Capital Regional District (CRD)

The regional government for southern Vancouver Island, 13 member municipalities. The CRD handles regional water supply (Sooke Lake / Goldstream), regional parks, the Hartland Landfill, recycling (contracted to GFL Environmental), regional sewage treatment (McLoughlin Point), and the regional housing trust.

Relationship with the Province

Municipalities in BC are creatures of the Province. The Province can amend the Local Government Act and Community Charter, mandate municipal action (e.g. Bill 44 on missing-middle), provide or withhold infrastructure funding, and in extreme cases dismiss a council.

Relationship with the Host Nations

The Songhees Nation and Esquimalt Nation are signatories to the Douglas Treaties (1850s). The federal Crown holds the formal treaty relationship; the City operates within the geography but is not a treaty signatory. The City CAN establish government-to-government working relationships, adopt Indigenous procurement standards, restore lək̓ʷəŋən place names with Nation consent, and co-fund initiatives. It CANNOT resolve federal Crown obligations, negotiate treaty terms, or speak for the Nations.

Statutory Frameworks

For candidates and journalists who want to verify what is in or out of bounds:

  • BC Local Government Act — general municipal authority, land use, elections, regional districts
  • BC Community Charter — corporate powers, taxation, bylaws, financial planning, council procedure
  • BC Police Act — municipal police boards and the Police Services Branch
  • Local Elections Campaign Financing Act — election finance, third-party advertising, contribution limits
  • BC Speculation and Vacancy Tax Act — provincial-only vacancy taxation

Victoria is not governed by the Vancouver Charter — that is a separate statute specific to Vancouver.

Common Misconceptions

  • "The Mayor can lower property taxes by signing an order." No. Rates are set by the Tax Rate Bylaw, requiring a majority vote, adopted by May 15.
  • "The Mayor can fire the Chief of Police." No. The Chief serves at the pleasure of the Police Board, of which the Mayor is one of several members.
  • "The City can ban opioids in Victoria." No. Drug scheduling is federal Criminal Code.
  • "If I sign a petition, my councillor has to vote a certain way." No. Councillors are representatives, not delegates.
  • "I can recall a councillor." No. BC has no municipal recall.
  • "In-camera meetings are illegal." No. They are statutorily permitted for specific topics (personnel, legal advice, real estate, certain procurement).
  • "City Council controls the police's day-to-day operations." No. The Police Board sets policy and budget; operational decisions belong to the Chief Constable.

How Residents Can Actually Engage

None of these is glamorous. All of them work better than complaining online.

  1. Write to all of Council. A short, specific, civil note about a current agenda item lands.
  2. Speak at a public hearing. ~5 minutes. Be specific. Bring evidence.
  3. Attend a Committee of the Whole meeting. Most policy issues are discussed here before going to Council.
  4. Meet your Councillors individually. The highest-leverage activity available to most residents.
  5. Submit on the budget consultation. Specific, costed, feasible submissions stand out.
  6. Join a Neighbourhood Association. Most have monthly meetings open to residents.
  7. Vote in every election. Municipal turnout is 35–45%. Your vote weighs about three times as much as in a federal election.
  8. Run for a position. The barrier is lower than people think.

A Checklist for Evaluating Candidate Promises

When any candidate — including any who has endorsed this framework — makes a commitment, ask:

  1. Is this within municipal authority? If not, the candidate should say "I will advocate," not "I will deliver."
  2. Is it costed? A promise without a budget is a wish.
  3. Is it sourced? A claim about results elsewhere should cite the city, study, or data.
  4. Is there a delivery date? "Eventually" is not a delivery date. "By Q4 of Year 2" is.
  5. Is there an accountability mechanism? How will you know if it's delivered?
  6. What does the candidate stop doing to fund it? "Find efficiencies" is not a source; "reduce administrative FTEs by 10% through attrition" is.
  7. Does the candidate know the relevant statute? A serious candidate has at least heard of the Community Charter and the Local Government Act.

If a candidate answers all seven well, they are doing the work. If they cannot answer half, they are running on aesthetics.

◆ A final word
Municipal government is the least glamorous level and the one that touches your daily life the most. It is the level most resistant to celebrity politics and most rewarded by competence. This page is what you need to read the statutes critically. The rest is up to you.
